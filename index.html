Foundations

Pain is your brain's best guess about danger, not a readout of damage.

Most of what we were taught about pain turns out to be wrong. Pain isn't a signal that travels from a hurt body part up to the brain like water through a pipe. It's something the brain produces when it decides protection is needed. This course covers the eight ideas that change how the rest makes sense.

Module 1 of 8
Pain is an alarm, not a damage report
Understand that pain is a protective output, not a measurement of tissue injury.

Stub your toe and the pain feels like proof something broke. It usually isn't.

Pain works more like a smoke detector than a damage report. The detector doesn't tell you how much of the kitchen is on fire, only that the brain has decided something might be wrong and you should pay attention. Sometimes the toast is just a little dark and the alarm still goes off at full volume.

Why it's unpleasant by design

The biological job of pain is to make you stop, protect, and learn, so you survive long enough to do it again tomorrow. A pleasant warning would be a useless warning.

The system evolved to err on the side of caution. The cost of missing a real threat (infection, a torn tendon, a predator's tooth) was death. The cost of a false alarm was a bad afternoon.

What this lens explains

The protective lens makes sense of pain experiences that confused the old wiring-diagram model:

  • Soldiers with serious wounds sometimes feel little pain in the moment, because survival demands action, not rest.
  • Paper cuts can feel sharp out of proportion to the tiny injury, because the brain treats the unexpected as more dangerous.
  • Phantom limbs hurt because the alarm system is intact even after the body part is gone.

Pain is not lying when it shows up without obvious damage. It's doing its job. The job is just more complicated than the old model suggested.

You don't argue with a smoke detector. You investigate, ventilate, and reset.

The next module breaks down the three flavors of pain that researchers now recognize, and why telling them apart changes what helps.

Pain = damage detected

A simple wire runs from a stubbed toe up to the brain, carrying a damage signal. The idea: tissue damage produces pain in proportion to itself.

Pain = the brain's protective output

The brain weighs many inputs at once, including tissue input, context, memory, and expectation, and then produces pain as a protective response. Damage is one input among many, not the whole story.

Evidence

  • Pain is defined by the IASP as an unpleasant sensory and emotional experience associated with, or resembling that associated with, actual or potential tissue damage (Raja et al., 2020).
  • Pain and nociception are different phenomena, and pain cannot be inferred solely from activity in sensory neurons (Raja et al., 2020).
  • Pain serves a protective function and is shaped by biological, psychological, and social factors (Moseley & Butler, 2017).

Myth check

Myth: If it hurts, something must be damaged. Reality: Pain reflects the brain's judgment about danger, which doesn't always match what's happening in tissue.

Module 2 of 8
Three flavors of pain, and why the label changes everything
Distinguish nociceptive, neuropathic, and nociplastic pain and recognize why the category guides treatment.

For most of medical history, pain was sorted into two bins: tissue injury (nociceptive) or nerve damage (neuropathic). That worked until clinicians kept seeing patients with real, severe pain and neither.

Kosek and colleagues proposed a third mechanistic descriptor in 2016, and the IASP adopted nociplastic pain as official terminology in 2017. The word looks intimidating, the idea is simple. Nociplastic pain comes from a nervous system that has become more sensitive than it should be, without clear tissue or nerve damage to explain it.

The three flavors

  • Nociceptive pain is what you'd predict from the injury. Sprain an ankle and the swollen tissue activates nociceptors. A burn, a cut, a bruise. It fades as healing happens.
  • Neuropathic pain comes from damage or disease in the nerves themselves. Shingles, diabetic foot pain, sciatica. It tends to feel electric, burning, or shooting, often along a nerve's territory.
  • Nociplastic pain comes from amplified processing without clear damage. Fibromyalgia, many cases of chronic low back pain with normal imaging, IBS, and complex regional pain syndrome often fit here.

A point worth naming: nociplastic pain is not the same as central sensitization. Central sensitization is a mechanism (amplified processing in the central nervous system) that can underlie nociplastic pain but also shows up in nociceptive and neuropathic states. Nociplastic is a clinical descriptor; central sensitization is one of its likely engines.

Nijs and colleagues developed criteria to help spot nociplastic pain: regional rather than discrete distribution, hypersensitivity to multiple kinds of input (light, sound, touch, temperature), and pain that doesn't track with physical activity in expected ways.

Why the labels change treatment

The treatments differ:

  • Nociceptive pain often responds to anti-inflammatories, load management, and time.
  • Neuropathic pain responds better to specific medications such as gabapentinoids or certain antidepressants.
  • Nociplastic pain typically responds best to graded movement, education, sleep work, and approaches that calm the nervous system, and tends to do worse with aggressive imaging-driven treatment.
Mismatching the treatment to the type is one of the main reasons people with chronic pain bounce through the system without getting better.

Many people have a mix of two or three types, which is normal and worth naming.

Evidence

  • Kosek et al. (2016) proposed nociplastic pain as a third mechanistic descriptor; the IASP formally adopted the term in 2017 to describe pain arising from altered nociception without clear tissue damage or somatosensory lesion.
  • Clinical criteria for nociplastic pain include regional rather than discrete distribution, hypersensitivity in the painful region, and comorbid features like fatigue, sleep, and cognitive problems (Fitzcharles et al., 2021).

Myth check

Myth: If imaging is clean, the pain isn't real. Reality: Nociplastic pain is real, common, and recognized by international pain bodies, and shows up with normal scans.

Clinical note

Pain type is rarely pure. Many patients have mixed pictures (e.g., osteoarthritis with central sensitization). These categories are a guide for matching strategy, not a diagnostic verdict. Confirming the dominant type, ideally with a clinician familiar with mechanism-based pain phenotyping, helps before committing to a plan.

Module 3 of 8
Pain is made in the brain (and that's not an insult)
Understand that pain is constructed by the brain from many inputs, not simply detected at the site of injury.

When people hear that pain is produced by the brain, they often hear something else: that the pain is fake, made up, or psychological in the dismissive sense. That's not what brain scientists mean.

Every experience you have, including the color red, the taste of coffee, the feeling of your foot on the floor, is constructed in the brain. The brain is the only organ that can produce conscious experience.

Tissues don't feel pain, they send information about chemistry, pressure, and temperature. The brain decides what to do with that information, and one option is to produce pain.

What the brain factors in

The decision uses more than the tissue input. It uses:

  • Context (where you are, what you're doing)
  • Memory (what happened last time something like this came up)
  • Expectation (what you think will happen next)
  • Mood, sleep, attention, and social signals

This is why the same blood draw hurts more when a stranger does it without warning than when your favorite nurse does it while chatting. Same needle, same skin, different pain.

Imaging confirms it's a network

Brain imaging studies show pain involves sensory areas plus regions handling emotion, attention, threat appraisal, and self-referential thinking. Wager and colleagues (2013) mapped what they called the neurologic pain signature, a pattern of brain activity that predicts experimental pain reports across people. The pattern involves a network, not a single 'pain center.' There isn't one.

The clinical implication: pain can be turned up or down by changing inputs the brain uses. Effect sizes for any single input are usually modest, and stacking several inputs over weeks tends to do more than any one alone.

A patient who learns that their persistent back pain doesn't mean their spine is crumbling often reports lower pain even before they start moving differently. A surgeon's confident reassurance, a placebo, a hand held by someone you love, a good night's sleep, none of these fix tissue. They change the calculation.

The pain is still produced in the brain. The brain just produces less of it.

Evidence

  • The neurologic pain signature is a brain-based biomarker that predicts physical pain across individuals using a distributed network of regions rather than a single locus (Wager et al., 2013).
  • Contextual factors including expectation, attention, and emotional state reliably modulate pain experience and corresponding brain activity (Bushnell, Ceko, & Low, 2013).
  • Pain neuroscience education (helping patients understand pain is produced by the brain) reduces pain and disability in chronic musculoskeletal pain (Louw et al., 2016).

Myth check

Myth: If pain is in the brain, it's imaginary. Reality: All experience is brain-produced. That doesn't make pain less real, it makes it more changeable.

Module 4 of 8
Nociception is not pain
Separate the body's danger-signaling activity (nociception) from the conscious experience of pain.

There's one distinction that, once you get it, makes most pain confusion go away. Nociception is the activity in your sensory neurons when they detect something potentially harmful. Pain is the conscious, unpleasant experience that sometimes results.

They are not the same thing.

Nociception without pain

You bang your shin against a coffee table while engrossed in a phone call and feel nothing until you look down and see the bruise forming.

The nociceptors fired. Your brain was busy. No pain was constructed.

Surgical patients under general anesthesia have nociceptive activity throughout an operation. They feel nothing.

Pain without nociception

The reverse happens too:

  • People with phantom limbs feel pain in a body part that no longer exists, so no peripheral nociceptor in that part is firing.
  • People with central sensitization feel pain from a light touch because the nervous system is amplifying input that isn't dangerous.
  • Stress and threat can produce pain in the absence of any new tissue input.

When the IASP updated the official definition of pain in 2020 (Raja et al.), it included a key note: pain and nociception are different phenomena, and the experience of pain cannot be inferred solely from activity in sensory neurons.

That single sentence quietly rewrites a lot of clinical practice. A clinician can't 'see' your pain on a scan. Tissue damage doesn't equal pain and pain doesn't equal tissue damage.

Asking the patient is still the most accurate way to measure pain, because the patient is the only one with access to the experience.

When your pain feels disproportionate to what your body has been through, you're not exaggerating. You're observing a real feature of how the system works.

Nociception and pain are linked but separable. The brain decides whether to translate one into the other based on dozens of factors, only some of which are conscious.

Evidence

The window where acute pain becomes persistent
Recognize the 3-month transition threshold (IASP/ICD-11), how it's defined, and the factors that shape whether pain resolves or persists.

Most pain is acute. You injure something, the tissue heals, the pain fades on roughly the timeline you'd expect.

When pain hangs around longer than the tissue should need, something else is happening. The current international standard, formalized in the IASP classification for ICD-11 (Treede et al., 2019), defines chronic pain as pain that persists or recurs for more than three months.

Why three months

Some older literature uses six months, and certain conditions are typically slower to resolve. We use three months here because that's the operative cutoff in the global classification system.

The number isn't magic. It's the rough point at which expected healing should be done, so persistent pain past that mark suggests the nervous system, and not just the tissue, is involved.

Sprains take weeks. Broken bones, six to twelve. Soft-tissue surgery, three to six months.

Who's at risk in the transition window

The window between three and six months is high-value for prevention. Linton, Nicholas, and Shaw (2018) catalogued well-replicated predictors of who's more likely to transition to chronic pain:

  • High pain intensity at the start
  • High distress and depression
  • Fear of movement (kinesiophobia)
  • Catastrophic thinking about pain
  • Low social support
  • Sleep disruption
  • Previous chronic pain elsewhere

Notice that very few of these are about the tissue. They're about the system processing the tissue.

Why aggressive workups can backfire

For non-specific musculoskeletal pain, early over-medicalization often makes things worse. Repeated imaging finds normal age-related changes that get blamed for the pain.

Strong opioids reduce pain in the short term but can sensitize the nervous system. Bed rest deconditions the body and confirms the brain's suspicion that the area is fragile. (When red flags are present, a different approach applies, see the red-flags module.)

The interventions with the best evidence in this window are often the least dramatic: reassurance based on a careful exam, education about how pain works, gentle graded return to movement, and treating sleep and mood.

When pain outlasts the healing schedule, the question shifts from 'what's still wrong with the tissue' to 'what's keeping the alarm system on.'

Evidence

  • Chronic pain is defined in the IASP/ICD-11 classification as pain persisting or recurring for longer than 3 months (Treede et al., 2019). Older literature sometimes uses a 6-month cutoff; this course uses the ICD-11 standard.
  • Psychosocial risk factors including catastrophizing, fear-avoidance, low recovery expectations, and depression predict the transition from acute to chronic pain better than tissue-based factors (Linton, Nicholas, & Shaw, 2018).
  • Roughly 10-40% of people with acute low back pain develop chronic symptoms, with the proportion strongly influenced by early management and modifiable risk factors (Hartvigsen et al., 2018).

Myth check

Myth: Pain that lasts must mean the injury never healed. Reality: Tissue healing is usually complete by 3-6 months, and ongoing pain reflects changes in how the nervous system processes signals.

Clinical note

Reassurance and graded movement assume red flags have been ruled out. New severe pain with weight loss, fever, history of cancer, IV drug use, neurological deficit (saddle anesthesia, bowel/bladder change, progressive weakness), or significant trauma needs medical evaluation before this protective-pain framing applies.

Module 6 of 8
More pain doesn't mean more damage
Dismantle the linear hurt-equals-harm assumption and replace it with a context-sensitive model.

Ask someone how badly they're injured and they'll usually answer with how much it hurts. That makes intuitive sense and it's wrong often enough to matter.

The relationship between tissue damage and pain intensity is loose. It's loose in the acute setting, where two people with identical knee injuries report wildly different pain. It's even looser in chronic pain, where pain can roar without any new damage at all.

The damage assumption fails in both directions

Studies of healthy adults with no back pain show that more than half have disc bulges visible on MRI by middle age, and the percentage rises every decade. Spinal degeneration is so common in pain-free people that the radiology community has stopped considering it diagnostic by itself. People with severe chronic back pain often have unremarkable scans.

The classic case study by Fisher and colleagues described a construction worker who jumped onto a 15-cm nail that went through his boot. He arrived in agony.

Removing the boot revealed the nail had passed cleanly between his toes. No tissue damage at all.

The pain was real. The damage assumption was wrong.

The reverse case is just as instructive. Every emergency department has stories of patients who walk in with a knife wound and underestimate how serious it is because they're not in much pain yet. Adrenaline, focus, and the brain's threat appraisal can mute even severe injuries until safety is reached.

Why this matters in daily life

People with persistent pain often interpret a flare as evidence that they re-injured themselves:

  • They restrict movement
  • They fear they're getting worse
  • The flare resolves and they conclude they got lucky
  • The cycle repeats and the world shrinks

The truth is more useful. Pain volume is set by the brain's protective decision, which can change with stress, sleep, mood, expectation, and a hundred other things. A bad pain day after a hard week often reflects a sensitized nervous system, not new damage.

Knowing pain isn't a damage meter doesn't make it less real. It makes it more workable.

Evidence

  • Disc bulges, degeneration, and protrusions are present in 30-84% of pain-free adults across the lifespan, making isolated imaging findings poor predictors of back pain (Brinjikji et al., 2015).
  • Pain intensity correlates only weakly with imaging findings in low back pain, knee osteoarthritis, and shoulder pain (Hartvigsen et al., 2018).
  • Striking case reports such as the nail-through-the-boot case (Fisher, Hassan, & O'Connor, 1995) illustrate that severe pain can occur without tissue damage and severe damage without immediate pain.

Myth check

Myth: A pain flare means I re-injured myself. Reality: Flares often reflect a temporarily more sensitive nervous system, not fresh damage.

The pain neuromatrix in plain English
Grasp Melzack's neuromatrix concept as a useful frame for understanding pain as a whole-system output.

Ronald Melzack spent his career working through anomalies the older theory of pain couldn't explain. Phantom limb pain was the big one. If pain comes from the body sending damage signals up to the brain, how does an amputated limb still hurt?

His answer, developed across the 1990s and refined since, was the neuromatrix theory. The pain neuromatrix is a network of brain regions that work together to produce what Melzack called a neurosignature, a unique output pattern, every time you experience pain.

The band metaphor

Think of it like a band. No single instrument is 'the song.' The song emerges from the whole group playing together. Take one instrument away and the band still plays, just differently.

The regions Melzack pointed to include:

  • The somatosensory cortex (the where)
  • The limbic system (the emotional weight)
  • The prefrontal cortex (the meaning-making)
  • Regions that handle threat appraisal, memory, and motor planning

Together, this network can produce pain even when the body isn't sending current input. That's why phantom limbs hurt. The neuromatrix has a stored pattern for that limb, and stress, memory, or stump activity can trigger the pattern with no limb to receive signals.

Why this matters for everyday pain

The frame helps explain why two people with identical injuries have different pain, why pain often worsens under stress, and why emotional and physical pain share so much neural real estate that brain scans struggle to tell them apart.

The neuromatrix is shaped by genetics, but also by experience. Repeated activation strengthens the pattern. This is part of why chronic pain becomes its own condition, the pattern gets practiced, like a song the band has played a thousand times.

If pain is a learned pattern in a network, the network can also learn quieter patterns.

Most modern pain treatments, from graded movement to pain reprocessing therapy, are essentially attempts to reshape the neuromatrix toward a less protective, less loud signature. The reshaping is real but gradual, measured in weeks to months of consistent practice.

Evidence

  • Melzack's neuromatrix theory proposes that pain is produced by a widely distributed brain network rather than a single pain center, with the output being a 'neurosignature' (Melzack, 2001).
  • The neuromatrix concept successfully accounts for phantom limb pain, an anomaly that earlier specificity theories of pain could not explain (Melzack, 2005).
  • Pain network activity overlaps substantially with networks involved in emotion regulation, attention, and threat processing (Tracey & Mantyh, 2007).

Myth check

Myth: There's a pain center in the brain that lights up when you're hurt. Reality: Pain is produced by a network of regions working together, with no single 'pain spot.'

Module 8 of 8
What this all means for you
Pull the foundational ideas together into actionable framing for the rest of the courses.

Seven modules in, the picture should look different than when you started. Pain isn't a damage report. It's an alarm built by your brain from many inputs, only one of which is what's happening in tissue.

The headlines so far

  • There are three kinds of pain that researchers recognize, and the kind you have changes what helps.
  • Nociception is the body's signaling activity. Pain is the conscious experience the brain may or may not produce in response.
  • Most pain resolves on a predictable timeline. When it doesn't, the question shifts from 'what's still broken' to 'what's keeping the alarm on.'
  • Pain intensity isn't a measure of how much damage you have. It's a measure of how much protection your brain has decided you need.

None of this is meant to make you doubt your pain. The opposite. Pain is real, every time, in every form.

The nail-through-the-boot worker felt real pain. The fibromyalgia patient feels real pain. The post-surgical patient under anesthesia has real nociception even though they feel nothing.

These aren't contradictions. They're features of a system more interesting than the old wiring-diagram model could capture.

Three takeaways for the next courses

  1. You can stop treating pain as a verdict on your body. It isn't. It's a vote, weighted by inputs you can influence.
  2. The categories matter. Pushing through fibromyalgia like it's a sprained ankle makes things worse. Babying a sprained ankle like it's fibromyalgia also makes things worse.
  3. Pain past the healing window isn't a sign that you're broken or making it up. It's a sign that the alarm system has gotten louder than it needs to be, and quieting it is a learnable skill backed by decades of evidence.

The next course goes deep on why persistent pain happens and what's actually going on in a sensitized nervous system. The course after that covers what to do about it.

Evidence

  • The biopsychosocial framing of pain has been adopted by major bodies including the IASP, NICE, and the WHO as the basis for chronic pain management (Engel, 1977; Gatchel et al., 2007).

Myth check
